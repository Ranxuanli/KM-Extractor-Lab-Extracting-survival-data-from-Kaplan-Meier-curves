Indirect Treatment Comparison module

Indirectly compare Treatment A versus Treatment C using evidence from A–B and B–C studies.

This page demonstrates a polished indirect-comparison workflow. For each pairwise study, the website can derive an approximate hazard ratio from reconstructed pseudo survival data. It then combines the two log-hazard ratios through a Bucher-style indirect comparison to obtain an approximate A–C estimate with a confidence interval and p-value.

Key assumption

The indirect comparison assumes the two studies are sufficiently comparable in terms of population, outcome definition, and follow-up structure for the shared comparator B to serve as a bridge. This is a teaching demonstration, so the website keeps the assumptions explicit rather than hiding them.

Bucher-style result

Approximate indirect estimate for A versus C

The website combines the two pairwise estimates on the log-hazard-ratio scale:

log(HR_A:C) = log(HR_A:B) + log(HR_B:C)
SE[log(HR_A:C)] = sqrt(SE_A:B^2 + SE_B:C^2)

Approximate p-value{{ '%.6g'|format(indirect.p_value) }}

Interpretation

If the hazard ratio is below 1, Treatment A appears better than Treatment C under the proportional-hazards approximation and under the assumptions required for an anchored indirect comparison. If it is above 1, Treatment C appears better. Because the inputs themselves come from approximate KM-curve reconstructions, the result should be described as exploratory or proof-of-concept.

Reporting language

Suggested phrasing for your write-up

In addition to the main pairwise-extraction pipeline, the website includes an extra-credit module for indirect treatment comparison. Two pairwise studies that share a common comparator can be entered or loaded from cached demonstrations. The site reconstructs approximate hazard-ratio inputs from Kaplan–Meier plots, combines them on the log-HR scale, and reports an anchored indirect estimate for A versus C with uncertainty intervals. The interface explicitly documents the comparability assumptions and the approximate nature of the reconstructed survival data.
